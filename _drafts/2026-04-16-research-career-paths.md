*Brando Miranda — April 2026*

Students take CS197 and then ask the same question at the end of the quarter: what now? You've learned to vector, to identify core and periphery, to deliver a number under uncertainty. Good. But what do you do with those skills after Stanford? This is the part of the course where I stop teaching methods and start talking about careers — because the methods only matter if you pick a path where they compound.

I'll be direct about my biases. I did a PhD, I work in a lab, and I think research is one of the most rewarding things a person can do. But research shows up in very different forms depending on where you sit, and the trade-offs are real. The goal here is to make them explicit.

## Four roles, four different jobs

When people say they want to "do research," they usually mean one of four things. Each has a different daily life, a different reward function, and a different way in.

A **professor** works on problems they find interesting, recruits and mentors rising talent, teaches in their area, and — if they're any good — graduates students who go on to do better work than they did. The outputs are publications, software adoption, and people. There's also real room for personalization: entrepreneurship on the side, speaking, consulting. It's one of the few jobs where the topic is genuinely yours.

A **research scientist** joins a lab inside a company — Microsoft Research, FAIR, Nvidia, OpenAI. The goal is research with impact, and that impact often translates into product. There's a story I half-remember about Microsoft Research: one of their projects, I think Kinect, worked out so well that it funded roughly thirty years of the lab's research. GPT-4 is doing something similar for OpenAI right now. That's the pattern — you do research, occasionally it becomes a product, and the product pays for the next decade of research.

An **entrepreneur** starts a company, usually on top of research they've already de-risked. You mostly know the technical idea works; the job is refining it into something millions of people will use. There's less research in the short term and more pitching, shipping, and scaling.

An **engineer** or engineering lead applies research-grade expertise to product. You're the person on the team who knows the thing nobody else knows — and many of these roles require levels of expertise that a BS or Master's doesn't get you. Short term, little research. Long term, enormous impact inside a product.

## What the distribution actually looks like

One of my TAs, Michael, scraped LinkedIn for keywords and plotted PhD outcomes from Stanford, MIT, and Berkeley. Roughly 20% become professors. The rest split across entrepreneurs, research scientists, and engineers, with engineers and research scientists forming the bulk — and within noise, those two are basically the same size.

The message I want students to take from that chart is not "don't do a PhD because most PhDs don't become professors." It's the opposite. A PhD routes you into any of the four roles. The professor pipeline is narrow, but the skills transfer. If you pick the PhD because you want to own a complex, uncertain project end to end, the career optionality is already good.

## How you actually get in

Every industry has a process. If you want to write books, you learn how publishers work. If you want to do film, you learn how LA works. Academia is no different — it just happens to be the one I know.

The short version: demonstrate interest and ability to do research, and get the paperwork that proves it. For undergrads at Stanford, that means getting involved early. Do a CS senior project. Take CS195. Work with a lab for academic year research or a CURIS summer project. Apply on time — the CURIS and PhD deadlines are not flexible. A BS with honors helps if your school offers it and you can keep the grades up.

One tip I repeat because it works: when you want to join a lab, don't cold-email the faculty first. Email the PhD students and postdocs. Read their websites. Look at what they're actually working on this quarter. They have the bandwidth to take you on, they know which projects need hands, and they can escalate to the PI once you've proven you can contribute. This is how most of the students I've mentored actually got started.

For the PhD application itself, you need three things: a statement of purpose, publications or project artifacts that show you can do the work, and three recommendation letters. The letters are the hardest part, because good letters only come from people who have seen you do research up close. That's why the undergrad research pipeline matters so much — it's the only way to earn the letters.

## Why the PhD, if you choose it

A PhD is a grown-up version of the CS197 project. You pick the vector. You pick the core and periphery. You own the publication. You learn to carry a complex, uncertain project over years instead of weeks, and fully own the outcome. It's rewarding and it's hard, and the skills I've been pushing all quarter — velocity and vectoring — are exactly the ones that get you through.

Do not do a PhD to be called "doctor." Do it because you want five or six years of protected time to think hard about something that matters to you. That's the real compensation.

## Takeaways

- **Four roles, one underlying skill set.** Professor, research scientist, entrepreneur, engineer — all draw on the same core ability to deliver under uncertainty. The daily life differs more than the skills do.
- **The distribution is wide.** Most PhDs don't become professors. That's fine. The training routes you into all four paths with real optionality.
- **Understand the process of the industry you want.** Academia runs on publications, letters, and deadlines. Learn the rules before you try to play.
- **Reach down before you reach up.** PhDs and postdocs are the right first contact in a lab, not the PI.
- **Pick for the work, not the title.** The title is the cheapest part of any of these paths.
