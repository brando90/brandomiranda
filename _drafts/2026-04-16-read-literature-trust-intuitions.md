*Brando Miranda — April 2026*

The standard advice to a new researcher goes something like this: spend a few years reading the literature, master the field, and only then start working on your own ideas. My advisor told me the opposite. Read a little. Trust your gut. Go.

That flip in sequencing changed how I work. I want to write down why I think it's right — at least for the kind of research I care about.

## The contrarian read

For some researchers, the "read for years, then build" path is probably correct. If your goal is to extend a well-defined subfield by one tight increment, you need the map before you walk. But if you want to do creative research — the kind where you suspect the field is pointing the wrong way — too much reading is actually a trap. You start to think in the field's grooves. You absorb its assumptions as gravity.

The move my advisor recommended is to read just enough to find the thing that feels wrong. Something everybody is doing, that you look at and your stomach says: that's not right. Then figure out how to do it right. And when people tell you it's no good — and they will — keep at it.

## Either your intuitions are good or they're not

The part I come back to most often is the simple decision rule behind this. Either your intuitions are good or they're not. If they're good, you should follow them — you'll eventually be successful. If they're not, it doesn't matter what you do. So you might as well trust them. There's no world in which ignoring your intuitions is the winning move.

I find this oddly freeing. It takes the meta-question off the table. You're not supposed to spend your twenties auditing whether you have taste. You're supposed to use your taste, ship the work, and let reality grade it. The feedback loop is the calibration.

This is also why criticism early on is not a useful stop signal. If the only thing keeping you going is the absence of pushback, your intuitions weren't really driving — consensus was.

## Don't just read — replicate

There's a second piece of advice that pairs with the first. Don't just read papers. Replicate them.

Replication is slow, which is the point. It forces a natural limit on how many papers you can absorb, and it changes what "reading" even means. When you actually try to reproduce a published result, you discover all the little tricks that were never in the paper — the initialization that matters, the preprocessing step nobody mentioned, the hyperparameter that was load-bearing. That tacit knowledge is the thing you're actually trying to acquire. Reading gives you the claims. Replicating gives you the mechanism.

## Takeaways

- Read enough to find what feels wrong. Not more.
- Contrarian taste is an asset. Don't sand it off with extra reading.
- The decision rule is simple: trust your intuitions. The alternative is strictly worse.
- Replicate, don't just read. The tricks live in the reproduction, not the abstract.
