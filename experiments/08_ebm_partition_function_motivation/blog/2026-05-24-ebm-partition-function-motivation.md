*Brando Miranda — May 2026 · ~5 min read*

**TL;DR.** Energy-based models have a beautifully direct idea: assign a scalar energy to a whole object, then turn low energy into high probability. The catch is the partition function. For one discrete variable, normalizing over all possibilities is cheap. For a length-`N` sequence, the normalizer ranges over `|V|^N` possible sequences. For continuous embeddings, it becomes a high-dimensional integral over `(R^D)^N`. This is the core tradeoff: EBMs can score whole configurations instead of committing to a left-to-right factorization, but the global normalization problem is exactly where the bill comes due.

---

The appealing pitch for an energy-based model is almost too simple.

Instead of predicting the next token, define a function `E_theta(x)` that assigns an energy to an object `x`. Lower energy means "more compatible with the data"; higher energy means "less compatible." Then define a probability distribution by exponentiating negative energy:

```text
p_theta(x) = exp(-E_theta(x)) / Z_theta
```

The denominator is the partition function:

```text
Z_theta = sum_x exp(-E_theta(x))
```

That one line is both the magic and the problem. It says: normalize the energy over every possible `x` so the probabilities add up to one. If `x` is a small discrete variable, fine. If `x` is a sentence, a proof, a plan, an image, or a trajectory, "every possible `x`" becomes the whole problem.

## The Toy Case

Suppose `x` is one categorical variable with vocabulary `V`. If `|V| = 10`, the partition function is just ten evaluations:

```text
Z_theta = sum_{x in V} exp(-E_theta(x))
```

That is not scary. Even if `E_theta` is a neural network, evaluating it ten times is a small constant. This is the regime where the definition feels like an ordinary softmax: score all options, exponentiate, normalize.

The same intuition survives if the energy is linear:

```text
E_theta(x) = theta^T x
```

For one-hot `x`, this is just selecting one coordinate of `theta`. You can dress the energy up as an MLP, but the structure is the same: one input, one scalar output, normalize over a small set.

This is the clean version people have in mind when they explain EBMs on a whiteboard.

## The Sequence Case

Now replace one token with a length-`N` sequence:

```text
x = (x_1, x_2, ..., x_N)
```

If each token comes from vocabulary `V`, then the sample space is:

```text
V^N = V x V x ... x V
```

The partition function is no longer over `|V|` objects. It is over `|V|^N` objects:

```text
Z_theta = sum_{x in V^N} exp(-E_theta(x))
```

This is the exponential explosion my handwritten note was circling. A vocabulary of size ten and a sequence length of one is ten options. A vocabulary of size ten and a sequence length of `N` is `10^N` options. Real language vocabularies are not size ten, and real outputs are not length one.

So the innocent-looking normalizer becomes:

```text
O(|V|^N)
```

That is the price of globally scoring whole sequences.

## Why Autoregressive Models Avoid This Bill

Autoregressive models make a different bargain. They do not normalize over whole sequences at once. They factor the sequence probability left to right:

```text
p(x_1, ..., x_N) = prod_t p(x_t | x_<t)
```

Each next-token distribution normalizes over `V`, not `V^N`. You pay `N` local softmaxes instead of one global normalizer:

```text
O(N |V|)
```

This is why autoregressive language models scale so cleanly. The model never asks, "Among every possible sentence, which one is best?" It asks, "Given the prefix so far, what token comes next?" That local decision is computationally convenient, easy to train with teacher forcing, and exactly aligned with the text data format.

The cost is that the model's probability is tied to a particular generation order. It commits to a prefix and then lives with the consequences. If a bad token enters the context, future predictions are conditioned on that bad context. That is the separate error-compounding issue. But computationally, the factorization is the trick that makes the normalizer cheap.

## What EBMs Buy

The reason EBMs remain tempting is that many objects are not naturally left-to-right.

A proof is not just a token stream; it is a globally constrained object. A mathematical derivation can be locally plausible but globally wrong. A plan can have every step look reasonable while the full trajectory violates the goal. An image can be corrupted by a global inconsistency that no single local patch explains.

An EBM can, in principle, score the whole configuration:

```text
E_theta(full object)
```

That is conceptually cleaner than pretending every object must be generated by a one-way chain of local conditionals. If the model can look at the entire candidate and say "this complete object is coherent" or "this complete object is incoherent," it gets access to a different kind of signal.

This is the intuitive motivation behind the note: EBMs are attractive because they let you define compatibility over whole sequences or whole configurations, not just local next-token moves.

But that motivation does not eliminate the partition function. It points straight at it.

## The Continuous Embedding Version

The discrete case is already bad. The embedding-space version is even more direct.

Suppose one token is represented by a vector in `R^D`. A length-`N` sequence of embeddings lives in:

```text
(R^D)^N
```

The partition function becomes a high-dimensional integral:

```text
Z_theta = integral exp(-E_theta(x)) dx
```

where `x` ranges over the entire sequence-embedding space. Unless `E_theta` has special structure, this is an arbitrary high-dimensional integral. There is no reason to expect a closed form. There is no reason to expect cheap exact computation. This is where sampling, contrastive methods, score matching, variational approximations, and all the other machinery enter.

The note says "high dimensional arbitrary function" because that is the core pain. The moment the energy is a flexible neural network, `exp(-E_theta(x))` is not a friendly Gaussian integral. It is just a complicated unnormalized density over a huge space.

## The Honest Tradeoff

The cleanest way to state the tradeoff is:

Autoregressive models make normalization easy by choosing a left-to-right factorization.

Energy-based models make global scoring natural by accepting an expensive normalizer.

Neither side is free. Autoregressive models are easy to train and sample from, but their local conditionals can compound mistakes over long horizons. EBMs can express global compatibility more directly, but exact likelihoods and exact sampling are generally hard because `Z_theta` is hard.

That is why I do not like arguments that present EBMs as a simple fix for autoregressive models. The EBM move changes the problem. It does not make the problem disappear. If anything, it makes the computational bottleneck more explicit.

For language, theorem proving, agents, and long-horizon reasoning, the interesting question is not "AR or EBM?" in the abstract. It is where we want the hard part to live. Do we want local next-token probabilities and a fragile rollout process? Or do we want global compatibility scores and a hard inference/normalization problem?

That question is still open enough to be interesting. But the partition function is the first place to look if the EBM story sounds too clean.

## TL;DR, Again

EBMs are attractive because they score whole configurations instead of forcing
everything through a left-to-right token factorization. The catch is the
partition function: for sequences, exact normalization scales over `|V|^N`
possibilities, and for continuous embeddings it becomes a high-dimensional
integral.
