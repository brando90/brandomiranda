## 1. One-Sentence Pitch

Prof. Sanmi Koyejo's STAIR group at Stanford proposes to build open benchmarks and agentic Lean workflows for evaluating and improving AI systems that produce formally verified mathematics and code, using Aristotle access and research support to advance trustworthy machine reasoning with deterministic proof checking.

## 2. Short Abstract

AI systems increasingly produce mathematical arguments, code, and scientific claims, but most evaluation remains informal: answers are checked by benchmarks, humans, or brittle tests rather than by proofs. We propose to develop open research infrastructure for trustworthy machine reasoning in Lean 4: benchmarks, proof-agent workflows, and evaluation methods that measure whether AI systems can turn informal specifications into Lean-checked mathematics and formally verified code.

The project will be led by Prof. Sanmi Koyejo's Stanford Trustworthy AI Research group, with Brando Miranda as a Ph.D. student research lead. It builds on Stanford-led artifacts including VeriBench, VeriBench-FTP, Pantograph, and Putnam-AXIOM, and aligns directly with Harmonic's Aristotle mission: accelerating AI and formal verification toward reliable mathematical reasoning. Support would fund student research effort, compute/API usage, benchmark infrastructure, and focused Lean workshops, with deliverables released as public datasets, evaluation harnesses, technical reports, and research papers where appropriate.

## 3. Longer Project Summary

Large language models are becoming powerful tools for mathematics, programming, and scientific reasoning, yet their outputs often remain difficult to trust. A model can produce a persuasive proof sketch, a plausible formalization, or code that passes ordinary tests while still failing under rigorous verification. Lean 4 changes the evaluation standard: claims can be checked by a proof assistant, and AI-generated artifacts can be accepted or rejected by a deterministic verifier rather than by style, confidence, or surface plausibility.

This project proposes to make that standard useful at research scale. Under Prof. Sanmi Koyejo's leadership in Stanford Trustworthy AI Research, we will develop open infrastructure for evaluating and improving AI agents that generate Lean-checked mathematics and verified code. The project will extend existing Stanford-led work on VeriBench, VeriBench-FTP, Pantograph, and Putnam-AXIOM into a more integrated research program: benchmark tasks, Lean environments, proof-agent interfaces, error taxonomies, and evaluation protocols for measuring progress in formal mathematical reasoning.

Harmonic's Aristotle models are a natural fit because they are designed for formal reasoning and Lean project work. Access to next-generation Aristotle models would let the team test frontier proof-agent capabilities on realistic tasks rather than only small static benchmarks. Research support would also allow the group to maintain high-quality benchmark infrastructure, support student researchers, run small technical workshops, and release artifacts that help the broader Lean and AI-for-math communities measure progress reliably.

The core scientific aim is to turn "AI for mathematics" from a collection of impressive demonstrations into a measurable, reproducible, formally checked research discipline. Success would be visible through public benchmarks, stronger evaluation protocols, Lean code and datasets, research papers, and a trained student cohort able to work across machine learning, proof assistants, programming languages, and formal verification.

## 7. Proposed Workstreams

### Workstream A: Verified Code and Theorem-Proving Benchmarks

Extend VeriBench and VeriBench-FTP into a cleaner evaluation suite for Lean-checked code and proof tasks. The goal is to cover multiple difficulty regimes: unit-level proof obligations, theorem-proving tasks, specification-to-code verification, proof repair, and end-to-end verified programming workflows.

Deliverables:

- Curated Lean 4 task suites with metadata, licenses, and reproducible environments.
- Baseline evaluations using available frontier formal-reasoning models, including Aristotle if access is granted.
- Error taxonomy covering failed formalization, invalid specification, proof search failure, dependency misuse, and incorrect code/proof coupling.
- Public technical report or paper.

### Workstream B: Agentic Lean Evaluation Harness

Build a reproducible harness for evaluating proof agents that interact with Lean projects over many steps. The harness should record trajectories, tool calls, failed states, successful proofs, dependency changes, and verifier outcomes. Student-built surfaces such as Conjecture Prover can serve as demonstration layers, but the PI-track deliverable should be the underlying reusable evaluation infrastructure.

Deliverables:

- Reproducible evaluation scripts and environment templates.
- Trajectory logs and metrics for proof-agent behavior.
- Integration experiments with Aristotle access, subject to Harmonic's terms.
- Documentation for external researchers to run or extend the evaluations.
- Public case studies showing where Aristotle-style agents succeed or fail on VeriBench-style tasks, with comparisons against existing Lean-agent baselines where permitted.

### Workstream C: Autoformalization and Statement Quality

Study how model-generated formal statements differ from intended informal problems and specifications. This matters because a theorem prover can verify the wrong theorem if the statement is weakened, mistranslated, or underspecified.

Deliverables:

- Statement-quality rubric for Lean autoformalization.
- Experiments connecting Putnam-AXIOM-style mathematical reasoning with Lean formalization tasks.
- Examples of failure modes where proof success does not imply semantic success.
- Draft paper or benchmark release.

### Workstream D: Student Research Infrastructure

Use support to train and coordinate students working at the intersection of ML, Lean, programming languages, and formal verification. Keep this as research infrastructure, not public sponsor advertising. Aristotle evaluation results should feed back into student project selection: which benchmarks need more coverage, which proof-agent failure modes are most important, and which Lean libraries or examples would most accelerate the community.

Deliverables:

- Internal Lean onboarding materials for researchers.
- Focused reading groups or mini-workshops on AI theorem proving and formal verification.
- Public artifacts when appropriate: tutorials, examples, datasets, and technical notes.
- Guided student projects shaped by concrete Aristotle evaluation results rather than generic interest in AI theorem proving.
